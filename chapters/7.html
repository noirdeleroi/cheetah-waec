1.7 Number Bases

Number bases represent the numerical system in which values are expressed. The base indicates the number of unique digits, including zero, that a numeral system uses. Common bases include:
  • Base 10 (Decimal): Standard numeric system.
  • Base 2 (Binary): Used in computer systems.
  • Base 16 (Hexadecimal): Common in programming and computing.
Key Concepts:
  • Converting numbers between bases involves successive division by the new base and noting remainders.
  • To perform operations on numbers in any base first convert them to base 10, perform the operation and then convert the answer back to original base.
Example 1: Conversion from Decimal to Binary \begin{align*} Convert 18 from base 10 to base 2. \\ Solution: \\ Step 1: Divide by 2 and record remainders: \\ 18 \div 2 &= 9 remainder 0,\\ 9 \div 2 &= 4 remainder 1,\\ 4 \div 2 &= 2 remainder 0,\\ 2 \div 2 &= 1 remainder 0,\\ 1 \div 2 &= 0 remainder 1.\\ Step 2: Write remainders in reverse order: & 10010_2. \end{align*} Example 2: Conversion from Binary to Decimal \begin{align*} Convert 1011_2 to base 10. \\ Solution: \\ Apply place values: \\ (1 \cdot 2^3) + (0 \cdot 2^2) + (1 \cdot 2^1) + (1 \cdot 2^0) & = 8 + 0 + 2 + 1 = 11. \end{align*} Example 3: Multiplication in Base 3 \begin{align*} Simplify 21_3 \cdot 2_3 in base 3. \\ Solution: \\ Convert each to decimal: & 21_3 = (2 \cdot 3^1) + (1 \cdot 3^0) = 6 + 1 = 7.\\ 2_3 &= 2.\\ Multiply in decimal: & 7 \cdot 2 = 14.\\ Convert 14 to base 3: & 14 \div 3 = 4 remainder 2, \quad 4 \div 3 = 1 remainder 1.\\ Result: & 112_3. \end{align*} Example 4: Squaring in Base 2 \begin{align*} Simplify: (11_{two})^2. \\ Solution: \\ Convert to decimal: & 11_{two} = 3.\\ Square in decimal: & 3^2 = 9.\\ Convert back to binary: & 9 \div 2 = 4 remainder 1, \quad 4 \div 2 = 2 remainder 0, \quad 2 \div 2 = 1 remainder 0, \quad 1 \div 2 = 0 remainder 1.\\ Result: & 1001_{two}. \end{align*}
